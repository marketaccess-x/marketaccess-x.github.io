Lynas Rare Earths: Why Policy Uncertainty Is the Least of Its Problems

03 February 2026 Critical Minerals Rare Earths Supply Chain Risk
U.S. policy favoritism toward domestic producers is real. But that's not Lynas's biggest problem.

In January 2026, the Trump administration invested $1.6 billion for a 10% stake in USA Rare Earth, making it the largest rare earth investment and fourth critical minerals equity deal. Commerce Secretary Lutnick framed the move as ensuring supply chains are no longer reliant on foreign nations. Combined with the Pentagon's earlier $400 million stake in MP Materials — including a $110/kg NdPr price floor — the policy signaling is clear: Washington favors wholly domestic supply chains.

For Australia's Lynas Rare Earths, which has the distinction of being the only commercial-scale rare earth producer outside China, this looks like marginalization. But U.S. policy favoritism may be the least of Lynas's problems.

The license that Lynas is operating under for its Malaysian processing facility is set to expire in March 2026. Environmental opposition persists given the over 1.2 million tons of accumulated radioactive waste. There is also historical precedent for Malaysia shutting down a rare earth processor before — this is exactly what happened to Mitsubishi's Bukit Merah refinery in 1992, which until today remains one of Asia's largest radioactive cleanup sites. If Lynas can't convince authorities to renew the license, it loses its only operational separation capacity.

The West's only scaled non-Chinese rare earth producer is one Malaysian regulatory decision away from crisis — and there is no backup plan.

Even assuming Malaysia renews, Lynas faces serious capacity constraints. In FY 2025 it produced approximately 6,600 tons of Neodymium-Praseodymium (NdPr — a crucial rare earth alloy used to create high-strength permanent magnets for electric vehicle motors, wind turbines, and electronics), but Japan holds offtake rights up to 7,200 tons plus 65% of heavy rare earths through 2038. This leaves Lynas with little to no spare volume with which to cultivate new markets.

The Texas facility in Seadrift that was supposed to provide additional U.S. capacity has likewise run into permitting failures, and Lynas has no finalized offtake agreement from the U.S. Department of Defense. Lynas's own public filings have flagged the planned Texas site as facing severe uncertainties.

The question for downstream manufacturers isn't whether Washington will support Lynas. It's whether Lynas can deliver at all. The West's only scaled non-Chinese rare earth producer is one Malaysian regulatory decision away from crisis — and there is no backup plan.
