The analytical problem this report solves

Washington has signed more critical minerals agreements in the past three years than in the preceding three decades. The February 2026 Critical Minerals Ministerial — 54 countries, 21 bilateral frameworks, the launch of FORGE as the MSP's successor, and a $12 billion US stockpiling initiative — is the most visible escalation yet.

The agreements are real. So is the gap between what they announce and what they can actually deliver. That gap is where supply chain risk actually concentrates. This report maps it precisely — and tells you what to do about it.

Key findings

Finding 01

Diplomatic velocity is real. Processing substitution is not. The current generation of agreements does not touch the midstream stages where China's leverage actually concentrates.

Finding 02

The implementation gap is structural, not incidental. Building processing ecosystems that rival China's requires decades of capital, engineering, and accumulated process knowledge.

Finding 03

The most underestimated risk is a strategic price war — Chinese state-linked producers expanding output at below-cost prices timed to coincide with allied project commissioning.

Finding 04

Demand-side governance shifts — procurement rules, traceability requirements, subsidy eligibility — may prove more durable than supply-side agreements and are already in motion.
